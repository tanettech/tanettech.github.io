About

Closing the digital language divide

I'm a Cameroonian researcher at Carnegie Mellon University working on multilingual speech systems for languages that modern AI has largely left behind. My work spans automatic speech recognition, speech-to-speech translation, and low-resource language modeling across more than 30 African languages.

My path runs from electrical engineering in Cameroon to speech technology at CMU, with deployment experience across Rwanda and Kenya. I care about the full arc of the problem: curating clean speech data, adapting transformer architectures to phonetically and morphologically rich languages, and shipping models that run on the resource-constrained hardware people actually use. Working across this stack taught me that for African language technology, data availability — not model design — is usually the real bottleneck.

I'm applying to PhD programs to pursue this research more deeply: robust, data-efficient speech and language models that serve communities historically excluded from AI.

Built speech translation systems for Bemba–English and Fongbe–French, surpassing prior benchmarks by 3% and 75% respectively through targeted model adaptation. Part of an effort to build multilingual speech-to-speech translation across 30+ low-resource African languages.

Multilingual ASR for 30+ African languages

Curated and standardized speech datasets for 31 African languages from public sources with privacy-protection standards and diversity across gender, age, and speaking context. Developed a competitive Kinyarwanda ASR model as the foundation for scaling to a multilingual architecture, addressing tonal and morphological complexity specific to these languages.
